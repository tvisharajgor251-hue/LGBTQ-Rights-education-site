Ancient origins

  • Early acceptance:

    • Some ancient cultures, like ancient India, had records of same-sex love and diverse gender identities. Texts like the Rigveda and Kama Sutra mention homosexual relationships, and ancient temples feature erotic sculptures depicting sexual fluidity.

  • Third-gender roles:

    • In some societies, like the Zuni people of North America, individuals with female identities were accepted in roles like Wewa, a male-bodied Zuni priestess who was also a weaver and potter.

  • Religious shifts:

    • The adoption of Christianity by the Roman Empire led to a cycle of alternating permissiveness and censure regarding homosexuality in some regions.

How Ancient it is

  1. Ancient and Early History
    • Mesopotamia: Texts sometimes mention homosexual acts; gods like Inanna had mythological figures with fluid sexual identities.
    • Ancient Egypt: Historical records suggest male-male relationships existed, though social norms around them are debated.
    • Greece: Same-sex relationships, particularly between adult men and adolescent boys (pederasty), were socially accepted in some city-states like Athens, often with a mentorship component.
    • Rome: Male-male relationships were common but framed around power dynamics; freeborn male citizens were expected to be dominant partners.
  2. Middle Ages to Early Modern Era
    • Europe: Christianization led to severe persecution of homosexual behavior. Sodomy was often punishable by death, but underground practices and coded social circles persisted.
    • Islamic World: Same-sex relationships existed in literature and art, though laws and societal norms varied.
    • Indigenous Cultures: Many Indigenous cultures in North America recognized Two-Spirit people, individuals embodying both masculine and feminine traits, and held them in spiritual and social roles.
  3. 19th Century ‚Äì The Rise of Modern Concepts
    • Sexology: The 19th century saw the birth of the study of sexual orientation and gender identity.
    • Karl Heinrich Ulrichs (Germany): Coined terms for same-sex attraction and advocated for legal reform.
    • Magnus Hirschfeld: Founded the Scientific-Humanitarian Committee (1897), one of the first organizations advocating for homosexual rights.
    • Gender Identity: Early discussions of transgender identities appeared, though often medicalized.
  4. 20th Century ‚Äì Activism and Visibility
    • Weimar Germany: Relative tolerance for LGBTQ+ communities; Berlin was a hub for gay culture.
    • United States & Europe: Homosexuality was criminalized, but subcultures thrived, often in bars, clubs, or artistic communities.
    • WWII: LGBTQ+ people were persecuted by the Nazis in Germany; pink triangles were used to identify homosexual prisoners.
    • 1950s: Formation of early homophile organizations like the Mattachine Society (USA) and the Daughters of Bilitis.
    • 1969 Stonewall Riots: Pivotal moment in New York City; considered the birth of the modern LGBTQ+ rights movement.
    • 1970s: Gay liberation movements expanded globally. Pride marches began, and activists fought for legal rights, healthcare, and social acceptance.
    • Trans Rights: Pioneers like Marsha P. Johnson and Sylvia Rivera were key figures in Stonewall and broader advocacy for transgender individuals.
